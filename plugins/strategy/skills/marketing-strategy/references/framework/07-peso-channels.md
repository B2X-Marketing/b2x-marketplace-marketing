# Layer 6: Channel & Media Mix — PESO Model

**Framework Source**: Gini Dietrich's PESO Model (Paid, Earned, Shared, Owned)
**Dependencies**: Layer 5 (RACE journey stages and tactics) and Layer 4 (SOSTAC budget allocation and tactical priorities)
**Produces**: PESO Channel Matrix integrated with RACE stages, budget allocations per channel, media mix strategy

---

## Why This Layer Matters

Knowing your customer journey (RACE) and your tactical priorities (SOSTAC) is not enough. You need to decide WHERE each tactic lives — which channels carry which messages at which stage of the journey. The PESO model provides a comprehensive lens for this: every marketing activity falls into one of four media types — Paid, Earned, Shared, or Owned — and each type has different cost structures, control levels, credibility profiles, and time horizons.

Most companies over-invest in one or two media types and ignore the others. A startup might rely entirely on Owned (website, blog) and Paid (ads), while neglecting Earned (PR, media) and Shared (community, UGC). The result is a lopsided strategy that is brittle — when one channel underperforms, there is no fallback.

PESO forces balance. It also forces integration: the most effective strategies have all four media types reinforcing each other. A blog post (Owned) gets picked up by a journalist (Earned), shared by customers (Shared), and amplified with ad spend (Paid). That is the multiplier effect.

## P — Paid Media

**Definition**: Any channel where you pay for placement — advertising, sponsorships, promoted content. You control the message and the timing, but credibility is lower because the audience knows you paid for it.

### Questions

Ask these conversationally, referencing the budget and tactics from Layer 4:

1. **Which paid channels make the most sense for your target segments?** Consider:
   - **Search ads (PPC)**: Google Ads, Bing Ads — capturing demand when people actively search for solutions. What keywords from Layer 5 (Reach) would you bid on?
   - **Social ads**: LinkedIn, Facebook/Instagram, Twitter/X, TikTok, Pinterest — which platforms does your target segment use (from Layer 2 psychographics)? What ad formats work best? (Feed ads, stories, video, carousel, lead gen forms)
   - **Display and programmatic**: Banner ads, retargeting, native advertising — are you targeting awareness (broad reach) or retargeting (people who already visited)?
   - **Sponsorships**: Newsletter sponsorships, podcast sponsorships, event sponsorships — are there niche channels where your segment concentrates?
   - **Influencer partnerships (paid)**: Are there influencers, creators, or thought leaders your segment trusts? What would a paid partnership look like?

2. **What is your budget allocation across paid channels?**
   - Total monthly paid media budget (from Layer 4)
   - How do you want to split it? (e.g., 60% search, 25% social, 15% retargeting)
   - Is there a minimum viable spend per channel? (Most PPC campaigns need $1,000-3,000/month to generate meaningful data)
   - Are you allocating budget for testing new channels, or concentrating on proven ones?

3. **What are your target economics for paid media?**
   - **CPA (Cost Per Acquisition)**: What can you afford to pay for a customer? (Work backwards from LTV and target margins)
   - **ROAS (Return on Ad Spend)**: What is the minimum acceptable return? (e.g., 3:1 means $3 revenue for every $1 spent)
   - **CPL (Cost Per Lead)**: What can you afford to pay for a qualified lead? (Work backwards from CPA and lead-to-customer conversion rate)
   - **CAC Payback Period**: How quickly must the customer pay back their acquisition cost?

4. **What creative assets do you need for paid media?**
   - Ad copy variations (how many? for which segments?)
   - Visual assets (images, videos, graphics — what formats and sizes?)
   - Landing pages (one per campaign/segment? or shared?)
   - Who creates these? In-house, freelancer, or agency?
   - What is the production timeline?

5. **How will you test and optimize paid campaigns?**
   - A/B testing plan (headlines, images, audiences, landing pages)
   - Optimization cadence (daily bid adjustments? weekly creative swaps? monthly strategy reviews?)
   - Attribution model (last-click, first-click, multi-touch?)

#### Follow-Up Probes

- If the user wants to advertise everywhere: "Spreading budget across too many paid channels means none of them get enough spend to optimize properly. Where does your target segment have the highest concentration and intent? Start with one or two channels and expand when they are profitable."
- If ROAS targets are unrealistic: "A 10:1 ROAS sounds great, but most industries average 2:1 to 5:1 depending on the channel and business model. What does your unit economics actually require for paid to be viable?"
- If no creative assets exist: "Paid media without strong creative is wasted spend. Before launching campaigns, you need at minimum: compelling ad copy tied to your brand script, 3-5 image or video variations for testing, and a landing page that matches the ad promise. Let's add creative production to the 90-day action plan."
- If paid is the only Reach tactic: "Paid media is a faucet — it turns on and off. What happens to your pipeline when you pause spend? Layer in Owned and Earned channels so you are not entirely dependent on paid."

## E — Earned Media

**Definition**: Coverage, mentions, and attention you earn through newsworthiness, relationships, and reputation. You do not control the message, but credibility is the highest of all four media types because a third party is endorsing you.

### Questions

1. **What makes your company newsworthy right now?** Consider:
   - New product or service launches
   - Significant funding, partnership, or acquisition
   - Original research, data, or industry insights
   - Contrarian or bold point of view on an industry trend
   - Founder story or company mission (your WHY from Layer 1)
   - Customer success stories with impressive results
   - Community impact or social responsibility initiatives

2. **Who are the target journalists, bloggers, analysts, and media outlets for your segments?**
   - Industry-specific publications (trade press, vertical blogs)
   - Business media (for company stories, funding, trends)
   - Technology media (for product innovation)
   - Local media (for community impact, hiring, events)
   - Analyst firms (for B2B — Gartner, Forrester, IDC, niche analysts)
   - Podcast hosts and newsletter writers in your space
   - Can you list 10-15 specific names or outlets?

3. **What is your current PR and media relations capability?**
   - Do you have a PR agency or in-house comms person?
   - Do you have existing media relationships?
   - Have you been covered by media before? What was the angle?
   - Do you have a press kit, media page, or newsroom on your website?

4. **What awards, speaking opportunities, or recognition programs are relevant?**
   - Industry awards (which ones? application deadlines?)
   - Speaking at conferences or events (which events does your segment attend?)
   - Guest authoring or contributing to industry publications
   - Analyst briefings or inclusion in reports

5. **How do you encourage organic word-of-mouth?**
   - Are your customers naturally talking about you? Where?
   - Is your product or service inherently "talkable" (remarkable, surprising, visible)?
   - What would need to be true for a customer to spontaneously recommend you?
   - Are there network effects or viral loops built into your offering?

#### Follow-Up Probes

- If the user thinks nothing is newsworthy: "Everything is newsworthy to someone. Your industry press cares about different things than business press. What data do you have access to that no one else does? What trend are you seeing that others are not? What opinion do you hold that is contrarian? Those are story angles."
- If there are no media relationships: "Earned media is built on relationships, and relationships take time. Start by mapping 15-20 journalists and outlets, follow them, share their work, engage with their content. When you have a genuine story, they will know who you are. Add media relationship building to the 90-day plan."
- If PR is being treated as advertising: "Earned media is not about pushing your product. It is about offering value to the journalist's audience — a unique story, original data, an expert perspective. The product mention comes naturally when the story is good."
- If word-of-mouth is passive: "Word-of-mouth is not luck. It is designed. What moment in the customer experience is so good — or so surprising — that they would tell someone about it? If that moment does not exist, create one."

## S — Shared Media

**Definition**: Social media platforms and community spaces where your content is shared, discussed, and amplified by your audience. You share control with the platform and the community. Credibility falls between Paid (low) and Earned (high).

### Questions

1. **Which social platforms are most relevant for each target segment?**
   - Reference the psychographic data from Layer 2 — where does this segment spend time?
   - **LinkedIn**: B2B decision-makers, professional services, thought leadership
   - **Instagram**: Visual products, lifestyle brands, younger demographics
   - **Facebook**: Community groups, local businesses, older demographics, B2C
   - **Twitter/X**: Real-time industry conversation, tech, media, opinion leaders
   - **TikTok**: Younger demographics, creative/visual, viral potential
   - **YouTube**: How-to content, product demos, thought leadership (long-form)
   - **Reddit**: Niche communities, authentic engagement, technical audiences
   - **Industry-specific communities**: Slack groups, Discord servers, forums

2. **What is your content strategy per platform?**
   - What type of content will you post on each platform? (Not all platforms get the same content)
   - What voice and tone? (Reference the brand script from Layer 3 — but adapted for each platform's culture)
   - What content formats work best on each platform? (Short video, carousels, long-form text, polls, stories, threads)
   - Will you repurpose content across platforms or create platform-native content?

3. **How will you encourage user-generated content (UGC) and sharing?**
   - What would make a customer want to share their experience with your product/service?
   - Are there shareable moments in your customer journey? (Unboxing, achievement, transformation, results)
   - Can you create hashtags, challenges, or templates that customers can use?
   - Do you highlight and reshare customer content?

4. **What is your community building strategy?**
   - Do you have or plan a community space? (Facebook Group, LinkedIn Group, Slack, Discord, forum)
   - What value does the community provide beyond your product? (Peer connection, learning, support, networking)
   - Who moderates and drives engagement in the community?
   - How does the community feed back into your marketing? (Insights, testimonials, content ideas, beta testing)

5. **What is your posting cadence and engagement plan?**
   - How often will you post per platform per week?
   - Who creates the content? Who manages the posting schedule?
   - How quickly do you respond to comments, DMs, and mentions?
   - How much time per day/week is allocated to social engagement (not just posting — actual conversation)?
   - What tools will you use? (Scheduling tools, monitoring tools, analytics)

#### Follow-Up Probes

- If the user wants to be on every platform: "Being mediocre on six platforms is worse than being excellent on two. Which platforms have the highest concentration of your target segment and the best organic reach for your content type? Start there."
- If strategy is the same across platforms: "Every platform has its own culture. A LinkedIn post that works will fail on Instagram. How will you adapt your messaging and format for each platform? Think about what the user is doing and feeling when they are on that platform."
- If there is no engagement plan: "Social media is not a broadcast channel. It is a conversation. If you post but never reply, never engage with others' content, and never participate in discussions, the algorithms will bury you. Who on your team is responsible for being present and responsive?"
- If UGC seems impossible: "Even in B2B, customers create shareable content if you make it easy. Can you create a template, tool, or framework that customers use and share? Can you feature customers in case studies or spotlight posts that they will reshare to their network?"

## O — Owned Media

**Definition**: Channels you own and control — your website, blog, email list, app, podcast, newsletter. You control the message, the timing, and the audience data. These are your most valuable long-term marketing assets because they do not depend on any platform's algorithm or ad auction.

### Questions

1. **What owned media channels do you have (or plan to build)?**
   - **Website**: Is it optimized for your target segments? Does the messaging align with your brand script? Is the architecture organized around the customer journey (RACE stages)?
   - **Blog/content hub**: Do you publish regularly? What topics? How does it perform in search?
   - **Email list and newsletter**: How large is your list? What is your open rate and click rate? How often do you email? What value does the newsletter provide?
   - **App or platform**: Do you have a mobile app or web application? Is it a marketing channel or purely a product?
   - **Podcast**: Do you have or plan one? What is the format and cadence? Who is the target audience?
   - **Video channel (YouTube, Vimeo)**: Do you publish video content? What type and how often?
   - **Other**: Knowledge base, resource library, online tools, calculators, assessments

2. **What is your content strategy for each owned channel?**
   - For each channel, what is the primary purpose? (Awareness, lead generation, nurture, retention, education)
   - What content themes or pillars align with your brand script and segment needs?
   - What is the publishing cadence you can sustain? (Be realistic — consistency beats volume)
   - Who creates the content? (In-house writer, subject matter experts, freelancers, AI-assisted)

3. **What is your SEO and content marketing investment?**
   - What is your current organic search visibility? (Key rankings, domain authority, monthly organic traffic)
   - What are the priority keyword clusters? (Map to segment JTBD and search terms from Layer 5 Reach)
   - What content needs to be created or updated for SEO?
   - What is the link-building or authority-building strategy?
   - What technical SEO work is needed? (Site speed, mobile optimization, schema markup, site architecture)

4. **What is your email marketing strategy?**
   - List segmentation: do you segment your list by customer type, stage, or behavior?
   - Automation: what triggered email sequences exist? (Welcome, nurture, onboarding, re-engagement, abandoned cart)
   - Personalization: how do you personalize email content per segment?
   - Growth: how are you building the email list? (Lead magnets, content upgrades, site popups, events)
   - Compliance: GDPR, CAN-SPAM, consent management

5. **How do your owned channels integrate with each other?**
   - Does the blog feed the email newsletter?
   - Does the podcast drive traffic to the website?
   - Does the website guide visitors to the email list?
   - Is there a logical flow from one owned channel to another?

#### Follow-Up Probes

- If the website is neglected: "Your website is your digital headquarters. Every other channel eventually drives people here. If the website is not aligned with your brand script, optimized for conversion, and organized around the customer journey, everything else underperforms. This may need to be a priority in the 90-day plan."
- If there is no email strategy: "Your email list is the only audience you truly own. Social platforms change algorithms. Ad costs rise. But your email list is yours. Building and nurturing it should be a foundational tactic."
- If content is sporadic: "The biggest challenge with owned media is consistency. One great blog post per month beats five mediocre ones. What cadence can you realistically sustain with your current resources — and what process ensures it actually happens?"
- If SEO is being ignored: "Organic search is the most sustainable source of qualified traffic. It compounds over time — every piece of content you publish is an asset that keeps working. Ignoring SEO means you are paying for every single visit, forever."

## Integration: RACE x PESO Grid

**Goal**: Map specific tactics into a grid that crosses RACE stages (Reach, Act, Convert, Engage) with PESO channels (Paid, Earned, Shared, Owned). This is the master tactical view — it shows exactly what happens where.

### Building the Grid

For each cell, identify the specific tactic, content type, or activity. Not every cell needs to be filled — some combinations are natural (Paid + Reach) and others are less common (Paid + Engage).

### Grid Review Questions

After building the grid:

1. **Balance Check**: Look across each row. Are you relying too heavily on one media type at any RACE stage?
2. **Budget Check**: Does the Paid column align with the budget allocation from Layer 4?
3. **Capacity Check**: Does the total activity level across all cells match your team's capacity?
4. **Gap Check**: Are there critical cells that are empty? Do they need to be filled, or are they strategically deprioritized?
5. **Integration Check**: How do the four columns work together? For example:
   - A blog post (Owned) gets shared on social (Shared), pitched to media (Earned), and amplified with ad spend (Paid)
   - A customer testimonial (Earned) becomes a social post (Shared), a case study (Owned), and an ad creative (Paid)
