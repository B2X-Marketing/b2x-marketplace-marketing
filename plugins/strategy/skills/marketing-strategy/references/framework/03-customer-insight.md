# Layer 2: Customer Insight — JTBD + Value Proposition Canvas + STP

**Framework Sources**: Jobs-to-be-Done (Clayton Christensen), Value Proposition Canvas (Alexander Osterwalder), Segmentation-Targeting-Positioning (Philip Kotler)
**Dependencies**: Layer 1 (Golden Circle) — uses WHY/HOW/WHAT to inform segmentation and positioning
**Produces**: Customer segment profiles, JTBD per segment, Value Proposition Canvas per segment, Positioning Statements

---

## Why This Layer Matters

Most marketing fails because companies talk about themselves instead of their customers. This layer forces a deep understanding of WHO the customer is, WHAT job they need done, and WHERE the company fits in their world. Three frameworks work together:

- **STP** structures the market into actionable segments
- **JTBD** reveals the real motivation behind buying decisions (not demographics — jobs)
- **Value Proposition Canvas** ensures the company's offering matches what the customer actually needs

---

## Sub-Phase A: Segmentation

**Goal**: Divide the total market into distinct groups that can be targeted differently.

Ask these questions referencing the WHAT from Layer 1:

1. **Who are your current customers?** Describe 2-4 distinct groups you serve (or want to serve).
2. **For each group, describe their demographics:**
   - B2B: Company size, industry, annual revenue, role/title of the buyer, role of the end-user (if different)
   - B2C: Age range, income level, location, education, lifestyle
3. **For each group, describe their psychographics:**
   - What do they value? What are their attitudes and beliefs?
   - How tech-savvy are they? How price-sensitive?
   - What media do they consume? Where do they spend time online?
4. **Are there customer groups you want to serve but currently do not?** Why not? What's blocking you?
5. **Of all these groups, which are:**
   - Most profitable (or highest potential)?
   - Growing fastest?
   - Most aligned with your WHY from Layer 1?

#### Follow-Up Probes

- If segments are too broad ("small businesses"): "Small businesses is millions of companies. Can you narrow it? What *type* of small business? In what industry? At what stage?"
- If the user only has one segment: "Even within that group, are there sub-groups that buy for different reasons or use your product differently? Those might be distinct segments."
- If segments overlap too much: "What is the *one thing* that makes Segment A fundamentally different from Segment B in how they buy or what they need?"

## Sub-Phase B: Jobs-to-be-Done

**Goal**: For each priority segment, understand the real job they're trying to get done.

**Key concept to explain to the user**: People don't buy products — they "hire" them to do a job. A fast-food chain discovered most milkshakes were sold before 8:30 AM. The job wasn't "dessert" — it was "make my boring commute interesting and keep me full until lunch." Your real competitors are whatever else could do that same job.

For each prioritized segment (limit to 2-3):

1. **What "job" is the customer trying to get done when they hire your product/service?** Describe the situation, not the solution.
2. **What is the functional job?** The practical task they need accomplished.
3. **What is the emotional job?** How they want to feel during and after.
4. **What is the social job?** How they want to be perceived by others.
5. **What are they currently using to get this job done?** Include direct competitors, indirect alternatives, and "doing nothing."
6. **What frustrates them about current solutions?** What's broken, slow, expensive, or painful?
7. **In what context or situation does this job arise?** The trigger moment — when and where does the need hit?

#### Follow-Up Probes

- If the user describes features instead of jobs: "That's what your product does. But what is the customer trying to accomplish in their life or business that leads them to your product? What happened right before they searched for a solution?"
- If the job is too abstract: "Can you describe a specific customer and a specific moment when they realized they needed this? Walk me through their day."
- If the user can't identify competitors: "If your product didn't exist, what would this customer do instead? That's your real competition — even if it's a spreadsheet, a manual process, or doing nothing."

## Sub-Phase C: Value Proposition Canvas

**Goal**: Ensure product-market fit by mapping customer needs against what you deliver.

For each priority segment, build the canvas:

### Customer Profile Side

1. **Customer Jobs** (carry forward from JTBD above):
   - Functional jobs: [list]
   - Emotional jobs: [list]
   - Social jobs: [list]
2. **Pains** — What annoys, frustrates, or blocks them?
   - What risks do they fear?
   - What common mistakes do they make?
   - What barriers prevent them from getting the job done?
   - What is the cost of the current problem (time, money, stress)?
3. **Gains** — What would they love to achieve beyond just solving the problem?
   - What would delight them?
   - What would make them look good to their boss/peers/customers?
   - What would save them significant time or money?
   - What outcome would they brag about?

### Value Map Side (map your offering against the customer profile)

4. **Products & Services**: Which of your offerings (from WHAT in Layer 1) serve this segment?
5. **Pain Relievers**: How specifically does your offering address each pain listed above?
   - For each pain, be specific: "Our [feature/approach] addresses [pain] by [mechanism]"
6. **Gain Creators**: How specifically does your offering create each gain?
   - For each gain: "Our [feature/approach] creates [gain] by [mechanism]"

### Fit Assessment

7. **Where is the fit strong?** Which pain relievers and gain creators are most compelling?
8. **Where are the gaps?** Are there significant pains you don't address or gains you can't create?
9. **What are the deal-breakers?** Any pains that, if unaddressed, would prevent the sale entirely?

## Sub-Phase D: Targeting & Positioning

**Goal**: Choose which segments to prioritize and define how you want to be perceived.

### Targeting

1. **Given the analysis above, which 1-3 segments should you prioritize?** Consider:
   - Strength of fit (Value Proposition Canvas alignment)
   - Market size and growth potential
   - Alignment with your WHY
   - Competitive intensity (how many others serve this segment well?)
   - Your ability to reach them (channels, budget, expertise)
2. **What is the priority order?** Primary segment, secondary, tertiary.
3. **Are there segments you should explicitly NOT pursue right now?** Why?

### Positioning

For each target segment, draft a positioning statement:

```
For [target customer] who [need/job-to-be-done],
[Company/Product] is a [category/frame of reference]
that [key benefit — the most compelling pain reliever or gain creator].
Unlike [primary competitor or alternative],
we [key differentiator — tied to HOW from Layer 1].
```

#### Follow-Up Probes

- If positioning is too similar across segments: "If the positioning is the same, these might actually be one segment. What would change if you spoke to Segment A vs. Segment B? The difference should be in the job, the pain, or the benefit emphasized."
- If "unlike" competitor is weak: "The 'unlike' clause is the sharpest differentiator. What would a customer who has tried [competitor] find genuinely different about you? Not slightly better — *different*."

## Connecting Forward

Layer 2 outputs feed directly into:
- **Layer 3 (StoryBrand)**: Each segment becomes a "hero." JTBD becomes the problem. Pains become stakes.
- **Layer 4 (SOSTAC)**: Segments define who objectives target. Positioning defines strategic direction.
- **Layer 5 (RACE)**: Journey is mapped per segment.
- **Layer 6 (PESO)**: Channel selection depends on where segments spend time.
