## Applicability

**This layer applies to consumer-facing and retail brands only.**

The P&G Who/What/How framework was designed for consumer packaged goods. It works well for B2C, direct-to-consumer, and retail brands where the end consumer is the target. It does not fit B2B engagements where buying decisions involve committees, long sales cycles, and solution-level evaluation.

**Routing**:
- **B2B engagements**: Skip this layer entirely. Proceed from Layer 7 to Synthesis (`10-synthesis.md`). The expanded coherence checklist in Synthesis absorbs the WHO/WHAT/HOW validation value for B2B.
- **B2C or consumer-facing engagements**: Run this layer in full, then proceed to Synthesis.
- **Both (B2B + B2C)**: Run this layer for the consumer-facing segments only.

If skipping, update the Strategy State: "Layer 8 (Strategy Validation) — skipped (B2B engagement)" and proceed to Synthesis.

## Why This Layer Matters

The P&G Who/What/How framework is one of the most enduring strategic planning tools in marketing history. Procter & Gamble has used it for decades to ensure that every brand in its portfolio has a clear, coherent strategy. The framework is deceptively simple — three questions:

- **WHO** is the target consumer?
- **WHAT** is the key benefit we are offering them?
- **HOW** do we deliver and communicate that benefit?

Despite being roughly 20 years old and originally designed for B2C consumer packaged goods, the Who/What/How structure is timeless. Every strategy, regardless of industry or business model, must answer these three questions clearly. If the answer to any one of them is muddled, the strategy has a problem.

In this framework, Layer 8 is NOT a primary planning layer. It is a **validation layer** — a sanity check that uses the P&G lens to look back at everything built in Layers 1-7 and confirm it hangs together. It catches inconsistencies, gaps, and misalignments that are easy to miss when you build a strategy layer by layer.

**This layer is optional.** It serves as a cross-check, not a primary input. If the strategy feels solid and coherent after Layers 1-7, this layer confirms that instinct. If something feels off, this layer helps pinpoint where and why.

## The P&G Framework (Summary)

At its core, the P&G Who/What/How framework requires strategists to complete three statements with absolute clarity:

### WHO

**"Who is the target?"**

This is not demographics alone. It is a vivid, specific description of the person (or company, in B2B) you are trying to reach — including their motivations, frustrations, behaviors, and decision-making context. The WHO should be so clear that any team member could recognize this person in the real world.

In our framework, the WHO is built from:
- Layer 2: Segment profiles, demographics, psychographics, JTBD
- Layer 5: RACE journey (where they are in the buying process)

### WHAT

**"What is the key benefit we are offering?"**

Not features. Not a list of everything the product does. A single, compelling benefit that the target cares about more than anything else. If you have to list five benefits, you have not made a strategic choice. The WHAT is the promise — the one thing that, if delivered, wins the customer.

In our framework, the WHAT is built from:
- Layer 1: WHY/HOW/WHAT (the Golden Circle)
- Layer 2: Value Proposition Canvas (pain relievers and gain creators)
- Layer 3: StoryBrand (the hero's success and transformation)
- Layer 2: Positioning Statements (key benefit and differentiator)

### HOW

**"How do we deliver and communicate this benefit?"**

The HOW covers two dimensions:
1. **Delivery**: How the product or service actually delivers the benefit (product design, service model, customer experience)
2. **Communication**: How we tell the target about the benefit (messaging, channels, content, creative)

In our framework, the HOW is built from:
- Layer 1: HOW differentiators (values in action)
- Layer 3: StoryBrand plan (the 3-step process)
- Layer 4: SOSTAC (strategy and tactics)
- Layer 5: RACE (customer journey and touchpoints)
- Layer 6: PESO (channel mix)
- Layer 7: Content Matrix (content plan and calendar)

## WHO/WHAT/HOW Summary Statement

For each target segment, draft a concise Who/What/How statement:

```
## Who/What/How: [Segment Name]

**WHO**: [Specific description of the target — not just demographics, but motivations, context, and current behavior]

**WHAT**: [The single key benefit — the one thing that matters most to this target]

**HOW**:
- Deliver: [How the product/service mechanism delivers the benefit]
- Communicate: [Key channels, content, and touchpoints used to reach and persuade the target]
```

**Test the statement**: Read it aloud. Is it clear? Is it specific? Could you hand it to a new team member and they would understand the strategy? If any part is vague, that is a signal to go back to the relevant layer and sharpen it.

## Gap and Inconsistency Resolution

For any item scored YELLOW or RED in the validation checklist:

1. **Identify the gap**: What specifically is missing, unclear, or inconsistent?
2. **Trace the source**: Which layer should have produced this answer?
3. **Determine the fix**: Does this require going back to a previous layer, or can it be resolved now?
4. **Document the resolution**: Note what was changed and why

Present any findings to the user:

"During the cross-check, I found the following items that need attention: [list]. For each one, here is what I recommend: [recommendations]. Would you like to address these now before we move to synthesis?"

## Historical Context

A note for practitioners: The P&G Who/What/How framework was developed in an era of mass consumer marketing — television advertising, retail shelf placement, and brand management at scale. Some of its assumptions (broad demographic targeting, single-channel communication, brand-level strategy) have evolved as marketing has become more digital, personalized, and multi-channel.

However, the core discipline — forcing clarity on WHO you serve, WHAT you promise, and HOW you deliver and communicate — is more relevant than ever. In a world of infinite channels and constant noise, the companies that win are the ones that can answer these three questions with precision.

This is why this layer exists as a validation check: not to replace the more detailed frameworks in Layers 1-7, but to ensure they all point in the same direction.
