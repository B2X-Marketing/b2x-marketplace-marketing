# Layer 3: Messaging & Story — StoryBrand SB7

**Framework Source**: Donald Miller's StoryBrand SB7 Framework
**Dependencies**: Layer 1 (Golden Circle) and Layer 2 (Customer Insight)
**Produces**: Brand Script per target segment, One-Liner, Messaging Hierarchy

---

## Why This Layer Matters

The single most important reframe in marketing: **the company is NOT the hero. The customer is the hero. The company is the guide.**

Most companies position themselves as the hero of the story — "We're the best, we're innovative, we have 20 years of experience." But customers don't care about the company's story. They care about their own story. They're looking for a guide who understands their problem and has a plan to solve it.

Think Yoda and Luke Skywalker. The customer is Luke (the hero with a problem). Your company is Yoda (the guide with the wisdom and the plan). StoryBrand uses the classic narrative arc to structure all marketing communication.

## Brand Script — Build One Per Primary Target Segment

Work through these seven elements in order. For each, explain the concept, then ask the questions.

### 1. Character (The Hero)

The hero is your customer, not your company. They must want something related to your offering.

**Questions**:
1. Looking at [Segment Name], what does this customer ultimately want? State it in one clear, simple sentence. (This should connect to the functional JTBD from Layer 2.)
2. Is this desire clearly something your company can help with?
3. Can you state this want in a way that creates a story gap — something they don't have yet but need?

**Guide for Claude**: The character's desire must be simple, relevant, and create tension. "Small business owners want to grow their revenue" works. "Small business owners want an integrated multi-channel marketing automation platform" does not — that's a solution, not a desire.

### 2. Problem

Every hero has a problem. StoryBrand identifies three levels of problem, plus a villain.

**Questions**:

**The Villain** (the root cause, not a person — a condition, force, or frustration):
1. What is the primary force or condition causing your customer's problem? (Economic conditions, complexity, outdated technology, information overload, time pressure, etc.)
2. Is this villain relatable? Would the customer immediately recognize it?
3. Is it real and specific — not abstract?

**External Problem** (the tangible, surface-level issue):
1. What is the obvious, practical problem your customer faces? (This is what they'd tell a friend they're struggling with.)

**Internal Problem** (how it makes them feel):
1. How does this external problem make your customer feel? Frustrated? Overwhelmed? Incompetent? Anxious? Behind? (This is the emotional JTBD from Layer 2.)
2. What self-doubt or internal struggle does this create?

**Philosophical Problem** (why it's just wrong):
1. Why *should* things be different? Complete: "It's just wrong that ___" or "No one should have to ___"
2. This should connect back to your WHY from Layer 1 — your belief about how the world should work.

#### Follow-Up Probes

- If problems are too product-focused: "Forget your product for a moment. What keeps your customer up at night? What would they complain about at dinner?"
- If the internal problem is missing: "The external problem is why they start looking. The internal problem is why they buy. What emotion drives the purchase decision?"
- If the philosophical problem feels forced: "Think about your WHY from Layer 1 — the belief that drives your company. How does your customer's problem violate that belief?"

### 3. Guide (Your Company)

The guide has two characteristics: empathy and authority.

**Questions**:

**Empathy**:
1. How do you show customers that you understand their struggle? What specific language, actions, or experiences demonstrate "we get it"?
2. Can you articulate their problem back to them better than they can describe it themselves?

**Authority**:
1. What gives you the right to guide this customer? Choose the most compelling:
   - **Credentials**: Awards, certifications, education, patents?
   - **Track record**: Specific results, case studies, metrics achieved?
   - **Experience**: Years in the field, number of clients served, specific expertise?
   - **Social proof**: Testimonials, logos, media mentions, reviews?
2. What is the single most impressive proof point you have? (One number, one story, one credential that would make someone trust you immediately.)

#### Follow-Up Probes

- If authority is all credentials: "Credentials establish competence but not connection. What's a story where you helped someone in exactly this situation? The specific story is more powerful than the credential."
- If empathy is generic: "Don't just say 'we understand.' Show it. What specific frustration can you name that your customer experiences daily? Name it precisely and they'll think 'they really get me.'"

### 4. Plan

The hero needs a clear, simple plan. Complexity kills action.

**Questions**:
1. What is your 3-4 step process for helping the customer? (Keep it to 3 if possible. Name each step in 2-4 words.)
   - Step 1: [e.g., "Schedule a consultation"]
   - Step 2: [e.g., "Get your custom plan"]
   - Step 3: [e.g., "See the results"]
2. Does this plan reduce confusion? After reading these steps, does the customer know exactly what happens next?
3. Does this plan reduce risk? Does it include any risk-reversals (guarantees, free trials, no-commitment offers)?

**Two types of plans**:
- **Process Plan**: Steps to engage with you (as above)
- **Agreement Plan**: Commitments you make that remove fear (e.g., "No long-term contracts", "Full refund if not satisfied", "We handle everything")

Which type — or a combination — works best for your business?

### 5. Call to Action

Every page, every email, every ad needs a clear CTA. StoryBrand distinguishes two types:

**Questions**:
1. **Direct CTA**: What is the ONE primary action you want the customer to take? (Buy, schedule, sign up, request a demo, etc.) This should be specific and unambiguous.
2. **Transitional CTA**: What is the lower-commitment step for those not ready? (Download a guide, subscribe to a newsletter, watch a video, take a quiz, attend a webinar, etc.)
3. Is your direct CTA visible and repeated across your marketing? Or is it hidden behind "Learn More" links?

#### Follow-Up Probes

- If the CTA is "Learn More": "That's not a call to action. That's a call to keep browsing. What is the actual action you want them to take?"
- If there are too many CTAs: "When you give people five choices, they choose none. What is THE ONE action that matters most for your business?"

### 6. Failure (The Stakes)

What happens if the hero doesn't act? This creates urgency without being manipulative.

**Questions**:
1. What happens if the customer does nothing? What stays broken, gets worse, or is lost?
2. What is the cost of inaction? (Money wasted, time lost, opportunity missed, stress continued)
3. Can you paint a vivid picture of the "before" state continuing indefinitely?

**Guide for Claude**: Don't use fear tactics. Use honest consequences. The goal is to help the customer recognize what's at stake, not to manipulate them. Reference the pains from the Value Proposition Canvas (Layer 2).

### 7. Success (The Transformation)

What does life look like after the hero engages with the guide?

**Questions**:
1. What does the customer's life/business look like after working with you? Paint the picture.
2. What specific transformation occurs? Map it:
   - **From** [current state] **To** [desired state]
   - **From** [feeling] **To** [feeling]
   - **From** [status] **To** [status]
3. How does success connect to the gains from the Value Proposition Canvas (Layer 2)?
4. What would the customer say in a testimonial six months after working with you?

## Artifacts

### 1. Brand Script (one per primary segment)

```
## Brand Script: [Segment Name]

**Character (Hero)**: [Who they are and what they want]

**Problem**:
- Villain: [Root cause / force]
- External: [Tangible problem]
- Internal: [How it makes them feel]
- Philosophical: [Why it's wrong]

**Guide (Your Company)**:
- Empathy: [How you show understanding]
- Authority: [Why they should trust you]

**Plan**:
1. [Step 1]
2. [Step 2]
3. [Step 3]

**Call to Action**:
- Direct: [Primary action]
- Transitional: [Lower-commitment step]

**Failure**: [What happens if they don't act]

**Success**: [The transformation — from ___ to ___]
```

### 2. One-Liner

The shortest possible version of the brand script. Format:

**"[Customer type] struggle with [problem]. [Company] provides [solution] so they can [success]."**

Test: Would someone hearing this for the first time understand what the company does and why they should care? Would they remember it an hour later?

### 3. Messaging Hierarchy

```
**Headline** (5-10 words): [Speaks to the hero's desire or transformation]
**Sub-headline** (15-25 words): [Identifies the problem and positions you as the guide]
**Body framework**: [Problem → Empathy → Authority → Plan → CTA → Stakes → Success]
```
